Common compounds and their application: Common compounds of thorium include thorium dioxide (ThO₂), used in high-temperature applications and gas mantles, and thorium nitrate (Th(NO₃)₄), which serves as a precursor for other thorium compounds and nuclear fuels. Thorium metal is utilized in specific alloys for strength and corrosion resistance, while thorium fluoride (ThF₄) is used in high-temperature applications and as an intermediate in other compounds.

Scientific use(s): Thorium is primarily used in scientific applications such as nuclear research, where it serves as a potential fuel for nuclear reactors due to its ability to breed uranium-233. Additionally, thorium compounds are used in various analytical chemistry techniques, including spectroscopic analysis and as catalysts in chemical reactions.

Commercial/Important uses: Thorium has several important commercial uses, primarily in the nuclear industry as a potential fuel for nuclear reactors due to its ability to produce uranium-233. It is also used in the manufacturing of gas mantles, where thorium dioxide enhances brightness and durability, and in high-temperature ceramics and alloys that require resistance to heat and corrosion.

Cost per 100-g: $142,500

Any other interesting facts: Thorium is more abundant in the Earth's crust than uranium and has the potential to be used as a safer and more sustainable nuclear fuel due to its less hazardous waste and long half-life.
